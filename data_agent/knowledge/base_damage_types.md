# Ski Base Damage Types — Identification, Severity, and Repair

## 1. Surface Scratch

**What it is:** A shallow mark in the base material (UHMWPE/polyethylene) that does not penetrate through to the core. The base material is intact; only the surface finish is disturbed.

**How to identify:** Run your fingernail across the mark. If your nail does not catch, it is a surface scratch. The base around it looks normal (black or colored), not white or dried out.

**Severity:** 1/5

**DIY vs Shop:** Always DIY — no repair needed beyond waxing.

**Estimated shop cost:** $0 (shops will tell you to wax it)

**Repair steps:**
1. Clean the base with a citrus base cleaner and a clean cloth.
2. Apply a thin coat of all-temperature wax with a warm iron (120°C).
3. Let cool 20–30 minutes, scrape tip-to-tail, brush with a nylon brush.

**Materials needed:** Base cleaner, universal wax, iron, scraper, nylon brush.

## 2. Base Gouge

**What it is:** A divot or channel in the base material that removes polyethylene but does not reach the wood or foam core. The base around the gouge may appear white/oxidized.

**How to identify:** The mark has depth — your fingernail catches. The surrounding base material is intact (no exposed wood grain or foam visible). Gouges are typically less than 3–4mm deep.

**Severity:** 2–3/5 depending on size. Small (< 5mm long, < 1mm deep): 2/5. Large or multiple: 3/5.

**DIY vs Shop:** DIY for most gouges with P-tex candle or ribbon. Shop if gouge is wider than ~8mm or very close to the edge (edge glue may be compromised).

**Estimated shop cost:** $20–$50 for base fill/grind depending on severity.

**DIY repair steps (P-tex candle method):**
1. Clean the area with base cleaner and dry completely.
2. Light a P-tex candle and let it burn for 10–15 seconds until it drips cleanly (no black smoke — black carbon weakens the repair).
3. Hold the candle 2–3mm above the gouge and drip molten P-tex to slightly overfill the divot.
4. Let cool completely (15–20 minutes minimum).
5. Use a sharp metal scraper at 45° to scrape the P-tex flush with the base. Work slowly — do not gouge surrounding base.
6. Finish with light sanding (220-grit) if needed, then wax.

**Alternative — P-tex ribbon with iron:** Lay ribbon over the gouge, cover with parchment paper, press with iron at 130°C for 10–15 seconds. Scrape flush when cool. Better adhesion than candle method for larger gouges.

**Materials needed:** P-tex candle or ribbon, lighter, metal scraper, sandpaper (220-grit optional), base cleaner, wax.

## 3. Core Shot

**What it is:** A gouge that penetrates all the way through the base material to expose the core (wood or foam). This is structural damage. Exposed core absorbs water, leading to delamination if not sealed.

**How to identify:** You can see wood grain or foam inside the gouge. The material at the bottom of the divot is not black polyethylene — it is lighter colored (cream/tan for wood core, white for foam). Pressing on the ski near the gouge may feel slightly soft or hollow.

**Severity:** 4/5 for a single small core shot. 5/5 for multiple or core shots near the binding area.

**DIY vs Shop:** 
- **Small core shot (< 10mm, away from bindings):** DIY with P-tex and extra fill layers is possible as a temporary fix, but a shop base grind is strongly recommended for a permanent repair.
- **Large core shot, multiple core shots, or any core shot under a binding:** Take to a shop. Core rot is a real risk. A shop will grind the base flat and apply a base weld.

**Estimated shop cost:** $40–$80 for a base weld and grind. $80–$150+ if delamination has begun.

**Temporary DIY seal (prevents water ingress until you can get to a shop):**
1. Dry the core area completely — use a hair dryer on low heat for several minutes.
2. Overfill the core shot with P-tex (candle or ribbon) in multiple layers. Let each layer cool before adding the next.
3. Scrape flush. The repair will not be as strong as a base weld, but it will seal the core.
4. Do not ski in wet/slushy conditions until repaired properly.

**Materials needed:** P-tex candle or ribbon, lighter, scraper, hair dryer, wax.

## 4. Edge Separation / Edge Crack

**What it is:** The steel edge separates from the base, creating a gap between the edge and the base material. This is a structural integrity issue and can cause the edge to catch and cause a fall.

**How to identify:** Run your finger along the edge. You will feel or see a gap between the steel edge and the base. In some cases, a section of edge has cracked or broken off. There may be rust under the gap.

**Severity:** 4–5/5. Any edge separation is serious.

**DIY vs Shop:** Shop only. Edge re-bonding requires specialized tools and adhesives. Attempting DIY with household super glue is not durable enough for ski forces and may make a proper repair harder.

**Estimated shop cost:** $30–$60 for a minor re-bond. Edge replacement: $100–$200+.

**Signs to watch for:**
- A "clicking" sound when flexing the ski tip or tail
- Visible rust trail along the base near the edge
- Edge that moves slightly when you press on it

**Safety note:** Do not ski on skis with edge separation. A loose edge can hook and cause a sudden fall.

## 5. Delamination

**What it is:** The layers of the ski (topsheet, core, base, or edge layers) separate from each other due to water ingress, impact, or age. Most commonly seen at the tip or tail, or spreading outward from a core shot.

**How to identify:** Press on the ski in different areas. A delaminated area will feel hollow or produce a dull sound when tapped (vs. a solid sound on healthy areas). You may see the topsheet lifting, bubbling, or cracks in the sidewall. At the tip/tail, the layers may visibly separate.

**Severity:** 3–5/5 depending on location and extent. Tip/tail delamination: 3/5. Mid-body or underfoot delamination: 5/5.

**DIY vs Shop:**
- **Minor tip/tail delamination (< 3cm gap):** DIY with epoxy is possible.
- **Anything else:** Shop. Spreading delamination that is not properly stopped will destroy the ski.

**DIY repair for minor tip/tail delamination:**
1. Carefully work the separated layers apart slightly with a thin tool (a dull butter knife). Do not force — you are creating space for adhesive, not widening the gap.
2. Clean between the layers with a cotton swab dipped in acetone. Let dry completely.
3. Inject or work in two-part epoxy (e.g., West System, Araldite). Do not use super glue — it is brittle under ski flex.
4. Clamp firmly with C-clamps or bar clamps. Place parchment paper between clamps and ski to prevent sticking.
5. Leave clamped for 24 hours minimum.
6. Remove clamps, trim any squeezed-out epoxy with a sharp chisel or scraper.

**Estimated shop cost:** $40–$100 for tip/tail. $150–$400+ for full delamination (often not worth repairing on older skis — replacement is more economical).

**Materials needed:** Two-part epoxy (5-minute epoxy is not strong enough — use 30-minute or structural epoxy), mixing tips, clamps, parchment paper, acetone, cotton swabs.

## 6. Topsheet Damage

**What it is:** Chips, cracks, or gouges in the cosmetic topsheet. This is typically cosmetic only, but large cracks can allow water into the core.

**How to identify:** Visible damage to the painted or graphic surface on top of the ski. The ski feels structurally sound — no flex changes, no hollow sounds.

**Severity:** 1–2/5 if purely cosmetic. 3/5 if cracks extend to the sidewall or are near the binding area.

**DIY vs Shop:** Usually DIY with clear nail polish or topsheet repair tape for cosmetic purposes. If cracks extend into the sidewall, shop repair is recommended to prevent water ingress.

**DIY repair:**
1. Clean the area with isopropyl alcohol.
2. Apply thin clear nail polish to seal hairline cracks. Let dry, apply second coat.
3. For larger chips: use clear or color-matched topsheet repair tape (available at ski shops) cut to size.

**Estimated shop cost:** $15–$30 for cosmetic topsheet repair.
