# Ski Binding Maintenance Guide

## Why Binding Maintenance Matters

Ski bindings are safety-critical equipment. Their sole function is to release the boot when forces reach a threshold that would otherwise cause injury, and to hold the boot firmly at all other times. A binding that is correctly set and maintained:
- Releases during falls to prevent leg and knee injuries
- Does not pre-release during normal skiing (a fall risk)
- Holds the boot securely through aggressive turns and impacts

Bindings that are poorly maintained, incorrectly set, or past their service life can fail in either direction — not releasing when they should, or releasing when they should not.

**Important:** Ski bindings are subject to indemnification rules in most countries. Only certified binding technicians (ski shop mechanics with manufacturer training) can officially certify a binding installation. If you adjust DIN settings yourself, you assume full responsibility for the outcome. This guide provides information for understanding your equipment — always have bindings inspected by a shop at least once per season.

---

## DIN Setting Overview

DIN (Deutsches Institut für Normung) is the standardized scale for ski binding release force. Higher DIN = more force required to release.

DIN settings are determined by a combination of factors:
- **Skier weight (kg)**
- **Skier height (cm)**
- **Boot sole length (mm)** — measured on the boot, not shoe size
- **Skier type (A, B, or C)**

### Skier Type Definitions
- **Type A:** Cautious, prefers lower speeds, less aggressive. Beginners.
- **Type B:** Average, intermediate to advanced recreational skier.
- **Type C:** Aggressive, fast, expert skier. Racers and advanced athletes.

*Most recreational adult skiers are Type B.*

## Forward Pressure Check

Forward pressure (also called "boot retention" or "heel tension") is the spring pressure that holds the heel piece against the boot. Incorrect forward pressure is one of the leading causes of binding pre-release or failure to release.

**How to check:**
1. Step into the binding with your ski boot.
2. Look at the forward pressure indicator on the heel piece (a small scale or window, usually on the side or back of the heel unit).
3. The indicator mark should align with the center of the adjustment range, or within the green zone if your binding has a color-coded indicator.
4. If the indicator shows too much or too little pressure, the binding needs to be adjusted by a technician — this requires the boot sole length measurement and a specialized tool.

**Signs of incorrect forward pressure:**
- Heel piece pops open when stepping in forcefully
- Heel piece will not stay closed during skiing
- Binding releases during normal turns (pre-release)

## Heel and Toe Piece Inspection Checklist

Perform this check at the start of each season and after any hard fall or impact.

### Toe Piece
- [ ] Pivot arm moves freely left and right (lubricant if stiff — use silicone spray, not WD-40)
- [ ] No cracks in the plastic housing
- [ ] Toe wings are not visibly worn or deformed
- [ ] Mounting screws are tight (do not over-tighten — use binding-specific torque specifications)
- [ ] AFD (anti-friction device) slides freely and is not cracked or missing

### Heel Piece
- [ ] Heel lifter (if present) snaps up and down cleanly
- [ ] Heel piece spring feels consistent from both sides
- [ ] Forward pressure indicator is visible and in correct range
- [ ] No cracks in housing or spring cover
- [ ] Power transmission plate (if present) is intact
- [ ] Mounting screws are tight

### General
- [ ] No rust on springs or moving metal parts
- [ ] Binding sits flat on ski — no rocking or movement when pressed
- [ ] Brake arms are not bent (brakes should retract fully when boot is engaged)
- [ ] No missing screws

## Release Testing

Test release before the first run of the season and after any significant impact.

### Lateral (Toe) Release Test
1. Step into both bindings.
2. On flat ground, twist your foot outward (fall simulation) with moderate force.
3. The toe piece should release before your knee reaches a painful angle.
4. If it does not release with moderate torque, DIN may be too high or binding may be stiff — consult a shop.

### Forward (Heel) Release Test
1. Step into bindings.
2. Lean forward and simulate a forward fall — push your shin hard into the boot tongue.
3. The heel piece should release.
4. If it does not, heel spring tension is too high for your DIN setting — consult a shop.

**Note:** Do not perform extreme release tests on the slope — always test on flat ground.

## When Bindings Must Go to a Shop

The following situations require a certified technician. Do not attempt to fix these yourself:

- **Binding does not release during release testing** — skiing on this equipment is dangerous
- **Pre-release during normal skiing** — binding is releasing too easily; injury risk from unexpected release
- **Any crack in the binding housing** — structural failure risk
- **Mounting screws stripped or missing** — binding can detach from ski
- **After a hard fall involving a release** — internal spring mechanisms can be damaged and may not reset correctly
- **Annual DIN certification** — most shops recommend annual function testing and DIN verification, especially for rental fleets and children's equipment
- **Binding is more than 10–15 years old** — manufacturer service life recommendations vary, but aging plastics and springs may not perform to specification

## Seasonal Inspection Checklist

**Beginning of season:**
- [ ] Clean all binding components with a dry brush — remove dirt and old lubricant
- [ ] Apply fresh silicone spray to pivot points and AFD
- [ ] Check all mounting screws with appropriate screwdriver — confirm torque
- [ ] Verify DIN settings match your current weight, skill level, and boot sole length
- [ ] Perform lateral and forward release tests
- [ ] Confirm forward pressure is in the correct range
- [ ] Inspect brakes — confirm they retract and deploy correctly

**End of season:**
- [ ] Clean bindings with dry cloth
- [ ] Apply silicone spray to all moving parts
- [ ] Release spring tension if binding has a travel/storage mode
- [ ] Store indoors in dry conditions — not in a car trunk or unheated garage

---

## Lubrication Notes

- **Use:** Silicone spray (e.g., Tri-Flow, Teflon-based lubricants)
- **Apply to:** AFD surface, toe pivot arm, heel pivot, brake pivot points
- **Do not use:** WD-40 (it displaces moisture short-term but leaves a residue that attracts dirt and degrades rubber/plastic over time), oil-based lubricants (attract snow and debris)
- **Frequency:** Once at the start of season; re-apply if binding feels stiff mid-season
